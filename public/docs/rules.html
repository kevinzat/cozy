Proof Rules Overview

Cozy supports a comprehensive set of inference rules for constructing proofs. Each rule can be used in forward mode (deriving new facts from known ones) or backward mode (breaking a goal into subgoals).

Forward vs Backward Syntax

In forward mode, you specify the rule name and reference the line numbers of its premises. In backward mode, the goal proposition is already known, so you provide less information (often just the rule name).

Reading the Tables Below Each rule shows its forward syntax, backward syntax, what it does, and an example. In forward syntax, L stands for a line reference; in backward syntax, parenthesized propositions are arguments you supply.

given

Introduces a hypothesis. Given lines appear automatically at the top of the proof from the theorem's hypotheses.

Forwardgiven (P and Q)
Backwardgiven
ProducesThe given proposition

cite

Cites a known theorem by name. The theorem must be in the allowed theorems list.

Forwardcite MultEqns
Backwardcite MultEqns
ProducesThe full statement of the cited theorem

repeat

Repeats a previously proven fact by referencing its line number. Useful for bringing a fact from an outer proof into the current context.

Forwardrepeat 3 or repeat ^1
ProducesThe same proposition as the referenced line

modus ponens

From A -> B and A, conclude B. Also known as implication elimination.

Forwardmodus ponens L1 L2
Backwardmodus ponens (A)
PremisesL1 = A -> B, L2 = A (or vice versa)
ProducesB

Example: If line 1 is P -> Q and line 2 is P, then modus ponens 1 2 produces Q.

direct proof

To prove A -> B, assume A and prove B in a subproof. Also known as implication introduction.

Forwarddirect proof (A)
Backwarddirect proof
CreatesA subproof assuming A with goal B
ProducesA -> B

intro and

From A and B, conclude A and B.

Forwardintro and L1 L2
Backwardintro and
PremisesL1 = A, L2 = B
ProducesA and B

elim and

From A and B, extract either A (left) or B (right).

Forwardelim and L left or elim and L right
Backwardelim and (A and B) left or elim and (A and B) right
PremiseL = A and B
ProducesA (left) or B (right)

intro or

From A, conclude A or B (left) or B or A (right). You must supply the "other" disjunct.

Forwardintro or L (B) right — proves A or B from A
intro or L (A) left — proves A or B from B
Backwardintro or (A) — to prove a disjunction, supply one disjunct
Left vs Right left means the supplied proposition goes on the left of the or; right means it goes on the right. So intro or 3 (Q) right where line 3 is P produces P or Q.

elim or

From A or B, A -> C, and B -> C, conclude C. This is disjunction elimination (proof by cases on an implication).

Forwardelim or L1 L2
Backwardelim or (A or B) left or elim or (A or B) right
PremisesL1 = A or B, L2 = A -> C or B -> C

cases / simple cases

Proof by cases. Given A or B, prove the goal assuming A and separately assuming B.

simple cases — uses two implication subproofs
Forwardsimple cases L1 L2
Backwardsimple cases (A or B)
PremisesL1 = A or B, L2 = A -> C or B -> C
cases — uses three lines: the disjunction and two implications
Forwardcases L1 L2 L3
Backwardcases (A or B)
PremisesL1 = A or B, L2 = A -> C, L3 = B -> C

contradiction

From A and not A, conclude false. Also known as negation elimination.

Forwardcontradiction L1 L2
Backwardcontradiction (A)
PremisesL1 = A, L2 = not A (or vice versa)
Producesfalse

absurdum

To prove not A, assume A and derive false in a subproof. Also known as negation introduction (reductio ad absurdum).

Forwardabsurdum (A)
Backwardabsurdum
CreatesA subproof assuming A with goal false
Producesnot A

exfalso

From false, conclude anything. Also known as ex falso quodlibet or false elimination.

Forwardexfalso L (Q)
Backwardexfalso
PremiseL = false
ProducesAny proposition Q you specify

verum

Conclude true with no premises. Also known as truth introduction.

Forwardverum
Backwardverum
Producestrue

tautology

Proves any propositional tautology by exhaustive truth-table checking. The proposition must be valid under all possible truth assignments to its propositional variables.

Forwardtautology (P or not P)
Backwardtautology
ProducesThe tautological proposition

Examples of tautologies: P or not P, (P -> Q) -> (not Q -> not P), (P -> (Q -> R)) -> ((P -> Q) -> (P -> R)).

equivalent

Replace a proposition with an equivalent one. Cozy checks equivalence by truth tables.

Forwardequivalent L (Q)
Backwardequivalent (Q)
PremiseL = some proposition P
ProducesQ, provided P <-> Q is a tautology

elim forall

Instantiate a universally quantified statement with specific expressions.

Forwardelim forall L {e1, e2, ...}
Backwardelim forall {e} x
PremiseL = forall x, P(x)
ProducesP(e1) (with variables substituted by the expressions)

Example: If line 1 is forall n, Even(n) -> Odd(n+1), then elim forall 1 {2*k} produces Even(2*k) -> Odd(2*k+1).

intro forall

To prove forall x, P(x), prove P(x) for an arbitrary fresh variable x in a subproof.

Forwardintro forall (forall x, P(x))
Backwardintro forall
CreatesA subproof with a fresh variable and goal P(x)
Producesforall x, P(x)

The subproof begins with "Let x be arbitrary."

elim exists

Given exists x, P(x), introduce a new variable c satisfying P(c) and continue the proof in a subproof.

Forwardelim exists L c
Backwardelim exists c x
PremiseL = exists x, P(x)
CreatesA subproof where c is a fresh variable and P(c) is known

intro exists

To prove exists x, P(x), provide a witness expression e and prove P(e).

Forwardintro exists L {e} x
Backwardintro exists {e}
PremiseL = P(e) (i.e., the body with the witness substituted)
Producesexists x, P(x)

Example: To prove exists k, n = 2*k given that n = 2*3, use witness 3: intro exists L {3} k.

induction

Mathematical induction. To prove forall n, P(n), prove the base case P(0) and the inductive step forall n, P(n) -> P(n+1).

Forwardinduction L1 L2
Backwardinduction
PremisesL1 = P(0), L2 = forall n, P(n) -> P(n+1)
Producesforall n, P(n)

substitute

Given an equation a = b and a fact containing a (or b), substitute to get the fact with b (or a) instead.

Forwardsubstitute L1 right L2 or substitute L1 left L2
Backwardsubstitute (a = b) right or substitute (a = b) left
PremisesL1 = equation, L2 = fact to rewrite

right replaces left-hand side with right-hand side; left does the reverse.

defof / undef

Expand or collapse a definition.

Forwarddefof Even L — expand the definition of Even in line L
undef Even L — collapse back into the predicate form
Backwarddefof Even / undef Even

Example: If line 3 is Even(n) and the definition of Even is forall x, Even(x) <-> (exists y, x = 2*y), then defof Even 3 produces exists y, n = 2*y.

apply

Apply a theorem by name. This instantiates a universally quantified theorem and applies modus ponens in one step.

Forwardapply TheoremName L
Backwardapply TheoremName
PremiseL = an instance of the theorem's hypothesis
ProducesThe corresponding conclusion of the theorem

algebra

An automated decision procedure for linear integer arithmetic. It can prove equalities, inequalities, and simple arithmetic facts using a combination of Gaussian elimination, the Simplex method, and Gomory cuts.

Forwardalgebra (n + 1 = m) L1 L2 ...
Backwardalgebra (premise1) (premise2) ...
PremisesOptional line references for facts to use as assumptions
ProducesThe stated arithmetic fact, if it follows from the premises

In forward mode, you state the conclusion and list any lines whose facts the solver should use. In backward mode, you list additional premise propositions.

What algebra can solve The algebra rule handles linear equations and inequalities over integers. It can prove facts like 2*k + 1 = 2*(k+1) - 1 or that an inequality follows from a set of known inequalities. It cannot handle nonlinear arithmetic (e.g., n^2 where n is a variable).
